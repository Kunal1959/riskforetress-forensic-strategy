Introduction
What Is Forensic Business Intelligence?

Most analysts look at what a company says. Forensic Business Intelligence is about finding what a company hides — not through illegal means, but through the careful, systematic analysis of publicly available data that most people ignore.

This document is your complete operating manual. It covers every data source, every red flag pattern, every research technique, and the exact format to produce a professional-grade Forensic Intelligence Report — the kind that makes investors, founders, and executives sit up and pay attention.

"The goal is not to destroy a company. The goal is to see clearly — and give that clarity to those who can act on it." — RiskFortress Principle

Chapter 01
The Forensic Mindset

Before you open any database or file, you need to think differently. Normal analysts ask: "How is this company performing?" Forensic analysts ask: "What is this company NOT telling me — and why?"

The 3 Core Questions
1.   What does the company CLAIM publicly?
2.   What does the DATA actually show?
3.   What is the GAP between claim and data — and who benefits from that gap?
The Peak Company Paradox

History shows that companies make their worst strategic mistakes at the height of their success. Peak performance creates overconfidence, blindspots, and resource misallocation. Understanding this paradox is the foundation of your research edge.

Chapter 03
The Research Process — Step by Step

Now that you know WHERE to look, here is exactly HOW to conduct a forensic investigation from zero to finished report. This is a repeatable process.

1
DAY 1 · 2 HOURS
Target Selection & Initial Hypothesis
  • Pick a company that is: (a) publicly visible, (b) recently in news, or (c) in a sector your client cares about.
  • Before starting research, write ONE sentence hypothesis. Example: "Byju's revenue claims do not match their cash burn pattern and talent exits."
  • This hypothesis keeps your research focused. You are trying to PROVE or DISPROVE it — not just collect data randomly.
  • Google the company + "controversy", "complaint", "fraud", "layoff" to check for prior public signals.
  • Check MCA immediately — is the company active? Are there any charges or director changes already?
2
DAY 1–2 · 4–6 HOURS
Financial Forensics
  • Download last 3–5 Annual Reports. Read the Notes to Accounts — NOT the headline numbers on the cover page.
  • Build a simple Excel: Revenue, EBITDA, Cash from Operations, Debt, Receivables — tracked across 5 years.
  • Look for divergence: Revenue going up while cash going down = revenue recognition issue. Always a red flag.
  • Check Related Party Transactions table — who is paying whom within the group? Any circular revenue?
  • Read the Auditor's Report carefully. Any "emphasis of matter" or qualifications? That is a major signal.
  • Compare Contingent Liabilities across years — if it grew 5x in one year, a major legal risk quietly emerged.
3
DAY 2 · 3 HOURS
Human Intelligence Sweep
  • LinkedIn: Search company, filter employees by "Past" — how many left in last 6 months? What seniority level?
  • Glassdoor: Read last 50 reviews chronologically. Note if sentiment changed sharply after a specific date.
  • Check the founder and C-suite LinkedIn activity — are they posting less? Speaking defensively? Attending too many events?
  • Search Twitter/X: company name + "not received", "scam", "delay", "where is my" — these are customer distress signals.
  • Check Indian Kanoon for all litigation. Case types matter: consumer cases = product issues. Labour cases = HR crisis.
4
DAY 3 · 3 HOURS
Competitive Gap Mapping
  • List the loopholes you have found. For each one, ask: which competitor is positioned to exploit this right now?
  • Research that competitor — do they have the capability to fill this gap? Are they already moving in this direction?
  • This is the intelligence product: Company X has loophole Y. Competitor Z can exploit it by doing A, B, C.
  • If writing for Company Z (your client), now you give them a specific action plan with timelines.
  • If publishing publicly, this section is what makes your analysis uniquely valuable — not just diagnosis but strategy.
5
DAY 3 · 2 HOURS
Verification & Cross-Check
  • Every claim needs at least TWO independent sources. If only one source says something — mark it as unverified.
  • Go back to your original hypothesis. Did the data prove or disprove it? Be honest — the best analysts change their mind.
  • Check if the "loopholes" you found are already known — search for existing analyst reports on the company.
  • Your value is in the CONNECTION between data points, not just listing individual facts in isolation.
  • Legal check: Are you using only publicly available information? Are you stating facts vs. opinions clearly?
6
DAY 4 · 4 HOURS
Report Writing & Distribution
  • Follow the Report Structure in Chapter 4 of this document exactly.
  • Lead with the most shocking finding — not background. Readers decide in the first paragraph whether to continue.
  • Use your own signature framework name for the analysis method — this builds your brand over time.
  • Publish on LinkedIn first (B2B audience). Then send directly to 10 relevant people: founders, investors, journalists.
  • Tag the companies you analyzed where appropriate — this drives massive organic reach.
Chapter 04
Report Structure — The Exact Format

A forensic intelligence report is not an essay. It is a structured document that takes the reader from context to conclusion with zero wasted words. Use this exact structure for every report you produce under the RiskFortress brand.

SectionContentLength
1. Executive Summary 3–5 sentences. The most important finding stated plainly. What company, what loophole, what risk, what opportunity. This is what gets shared on social media. Half page
2. Company Overview Brief factual context. Founded when, what they do, current valuation/revenue, recent news. No opinion here. Just facts to orient the reader. Half page
3. Methodology State your sources and process. "This analysis is based on MCA filings, 3 years of annual reports, 200 Glassdoor reviews, and LinkedIn employee data." This builds credibility immediately. Quarter page
4. Key Findings The core of the report. 3–5 specific loopholes with evidence. Each finding: what it is, data that proves it, why it matters, when it started. No opinions — only data-backed observations. 2–3 pages
5. Risk Quantification Put a number on each loophole. "If receivables are not collected, ₹400Cr is at risk." Numbers make findings real and shareable. 1 page
6. Competitive Opportunity Map For each loophole — which competitor benefits, and how. Specific and actionable. "Competitor X should target Y's enterprise clients in the next 90 days while Y is distracted with restructuring." 1 page
7. Conclusion & Recommendations What should happen next. If writing for a client — their action plan. If publishing — your prediction. End with a strong, quotable sentence. Half page
8. Disclaimer "This report is based solely on publicly available information. All opinions are the analyst's own. This is not investment advice." 3–4 lines
Chapter 05
Competitive Opportunity — Loopholes Into Strategy

This chapter is what separates a journalist from a strategist. Journalists expose loopholes. Strategists show who benefits — and exactly how. This is where RiskFortress creates the most value for clients.

The Loophole-to-Opportunity Matrix
Loophole TypeWhat It SignalsCompetitor Action to Recommend
Key Talent Exiting
(C-suite / Product heads)		 Institutional knowledge leaving. Product roadmap will slow. Customer relationships at risk. Poach those executives immediately. Hire them before competitors do. They carry client relationships and product context.
Customer Complaints Spiking
(App reviews, Twitter, Consumer Court)		 NPS declining. Churn about to increase. Enterprise clients looking for alternatives. Run targeted ads to that company's unhappy customers. Create migration offer. Reach out directly to their enterprise accounts.
Funding Gap
(Last round 18+ months ago)		 Company will cut costs, may miss product commitments, may lose key team members. Approach their clients proactively with a stability narrative. "We have 3 years of runway. They don't."
Regulatory / Legal Issues
(NCLT, SEBI action, consumer forum)		 Management distracted by legal battles. Brand reputation at risk. Clients getting nervous. Reach out to their clients with a risk mitigation pitch. "When your vendor is under legal scrutiny, your data is at risk."
Geographic Overexpansion
(10 new cities in 12 months)		 Capital being stretched thin. Unit economics in new markets likely negative. Core market being neglected. Dominate the core market they are neglecting. Increase spend where they have reduced presence.
Product Quality Degradation
(Reviews and support tickets)		 Engineering team stretched or departing. Tech debt accumulating. Enterprise clients will not renew. Launch enterprise-grade reliability campaign. Target renewal cycle of their top clients — 60 days before contract end.
The 90-Day Exploitation Window

Most loopholes have a 90-day window before the company either fixes them or before the market already knows. Your report must always include a time-sensitive recommendation. This is what makes clients act — urgency backed by evidence.

Template sentence for every report:

"Based on current talent exit velocity and funding timeline, Company X has approximately 60–90 days before their enterprise product quality visibly degrades. Competitor action must begin within 30 days to capture maximum opportunity."
